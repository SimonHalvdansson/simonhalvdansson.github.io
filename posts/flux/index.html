Introducing Flux, and some thoughts on agentic coding

By Simon Halvdansson | Apr. 2026

I just finished version 1.0 of Flux, an electricity price tracking app for Android, and wanted to put down some words about the development process along with some assorted musings about agentic coding. This post is less structured than usual and is rather and attempt by me to sort out some thought on the state of the industry.

Figure: Screenshots from Flux running on a phone.

Since the energy crisis in Europe around 2022, and especially since starting at SINTEF Energy Research, I have been thinking a lot about energy costs and supplies. Nowadays energy availability is especially important the context of AI data centers, although the extent of this is probably a good Rorschach test for a persons views on a possible AGI scenario. Either way, consumer awareness of energy prices appears to still be at a high level and there is some room to save by optimizing usage to time local spot markets in Europe.

If you generally enjoy optimizing things, timing the charging of your car with this market can be a fun activity, and evening out demand generally has a positive impact on emissions. I am such a person, but I also take great pleasure in good design and have a hard time putting up with UIs that don't look good. This was the situation for the Android electricity price apps, at least in my opinion, about a year ago when I started work on Flux.

Figure: Various competing electricity price apps for Android.

So the goal was to design and develop a simple solution to this problem, preferably with a homescreen widget since going into an app to check prices is even more of a commitment.

Development

In these pre-Codex/CC days of early 2025, at least I was still copy-pasting from ChatGPT whenever I had it write something for me. This is how an initial version of the app was built, with only a widget and an unreliable implementation of the prices API from hvakosterstrommen.no/. This is a rather natural place for a passion project of this kind to end up, abandoned after reaching some sort of MVP status.

As a TODO list sat untouched for an increasing number of months, GPT-5 was launched and the first version of Codex on the web. I learned a lot iterating on Flux using Codex as I gave up the idea of maintaining an understanding of the codebase and instead embraced the exponential. While I by no means think this is how all software should be developed, I think it is very easy to dismiss without second thought for reasons which are seemingly obvious.

Ultimately, it is impossible to truly know at which point the returns will start to diminish. I think the next year or so will bring an ever increased focus on taste as this is harder to verify than correctness, albeit far from impossible. Flux is by no means a pinnacle of taste but iterating in the modern fashion makes the link between quality and taste painfully obvious, what you ask for is what you get.

I've yet to use the MCP tools for end-to-end iteration on Android apps for Flux development, although I did try it out with Gemini 3 Flash in Android Studio for a separate project. From that experience, I have no doubt that Flux could have been developed in a considerably shortened timeline today. Perhaps this is what will bring the promised explosion of software, of which I view Flux as a part of.
